Case Study: The "Payment Request" TrapThe Scenario: A Digital Ambush
Imagine Aryan, a first-year B.Tech student at VIT Bhopal, trying to sell his old bicycle on a popular online marketplace to save up for a new laptop. 
Within thirty minutes of posting the ad, he receives a WhatsApp message from a "buyer" claiming to be an army officer stationed at a remote base. The buyer seems urgent and professional, offering to pay the full price immediately via UPI to "lock in" the deal before anyone else buys it.How the Crime Unfolds: Step-by-Step
The attacker sends Aryan a QR code over the chat, claiming, "I have sent the payment, you just need to scan this to receive the money into your bank account". When Aryan scans the code, his UPI app opens automatically. Instead of a "Receive" button, the screen shows a "Pay" or "Confirm" button and asks for his 4-digit security PIN.The attacker stays on the phone with Aryan, using high-pressure tactics, the link will expire in 60 seconds! Just enter the PIN to authorize the receipt. Confused and rushed, Aryan enters his PIN. Instantly, his phone buzzes—not with a credit notification, but with a message stating that ₹5,000 has been debited from his account. The attacker had sent a "Request Money" notification rather than a transfer. By entering his PIN, Aryan unintentionally authorized a payment to the scammer.Target Audience and Impact
College students are primary targets because they are frequent users of digital payment apps and often engage in peer-to-peer buying and selling. For a student, the consequences are devastating: the loss of a month’s pocket money or mess fees happens in seconds. Because UPI transfers are instantaneous and final, recovering the funds through a bank is extremely difficult once the transaction is authorized by a PIN.Lessons for the Digital Ambassador
This case highlights a fundamental rule of digital literacy: You never need to enter your UPI PIN to receive money. It shows that scammers don't just use technical bugs, they use "Social Engineering"—psychological tricks like urgency and fake authority—to make smart people make mistakes.
